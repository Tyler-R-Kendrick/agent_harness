# Notte Marketing

## Audience

- Developers building browser agents, web automation APIs, scraping systems, and authenticated web workflows.
- AI companies that need browser infrastructure, agent execution, serverless functions, and credential primitives in one platform.
- Teams trying to reduce model costs by moving from pure runtime agents toward action APIs and reusable functions.

## Positioning

- Core claim: browser infrastructure that lets AI run on the internet at speed.
- Market frame: remote browsers alone are not enough; agents also need perception, authentication, identities, serverless runtime, and observability.
- Product promise: build, run, debug, and deploy browser automations through sessions, agents, functions, vaults, identities, and Studio.

## Customer Model

- Free plan for initial adoption, then paid developer/startup/enterprise plans.
- Usage pricing includes browser hours, proxy bandwidth, and pass-through LLM token cost.
- Enterprise expansion comes from concurrency, advanced stealth, BYO keys/proxies/cloud, and auth/identity primitives.

## Who They Capture

- AI-agent startups that want a complete browser stack instead of stitching together Browserbase, Browser Use, auth vaults, workers, and observability.
- Developers who want Playwright-compatible sessions but also higher-level agent and action APIs.
- Teams that need authenticated agents to behave like real users across websites.

## Who They Miss

- End users who want a browser product rather than an API platform.
- Teams with strict local-only requirements.
- Buyers skeptical of broad platform claims or benchmark-driven positioning.

## Competitive Read

Notte pressures `agent-browser` on platform completeness, especially around auth, identities, and "agent to function" deployment. `agent-browser` can counter with transparent local execution, workspace-native traces, and human-in-the-loop evidence review rather than cloud platform breadth.
