# Browser Operator Marketing

## Audience

- Users who want an AI browser but prefer open-source and local execution.
- Developers and power users comparing ChatGPT Atlas, Perplexity Comet, Dia, and Edge Copilot Mode.
- Researchers who want a browser agent they can watch and steer.
- Model-provider tinkerers who care about OpenRouter, OpenAI, Groq, LiteLLM, or local models.

## Positioning

Browser Operator positions itself as the open-source, privacy-focused AI browser. The strongest message is control: the browser runs locally, the user chooses the provider, and the agent works in a visible browser instead of a remote black box.

## Customer Model

- Open-source distribution through GitHub and direct desktop downloads.
- Community-led growth through Discord, GitHub stars, releases, and docs.
- No obvious enterprise procurement motion yet; the product currently reads as an enthusiast and early-adopter browser.

## Who It Captures

- AI-browser curious users who do not want a model-owner browser as their primary surface.
- Local-first users who want to keep browser execution on their machine.
- Developers willing to configure providers and tolerate young-product rough edges.

## Who It Does Not Capture

- Enterprises that require managed policy, audit export, SSO, and vendor-backed support.
- Non-technical users who do not understand provider setup or local model proxies.
- Linux-first users if platform support remains incomplete.
- Buyers who need deterministic run records, approvals, and replayable evidence before trusting autonomous browsing.

## Competitive Takeaway

Browser Operator validates the local AI-browser wedge, but `agent-browser` can compete by making traces, approvals, security boundaries, and recovery controls more explicit than a general-purpose browser shell.
