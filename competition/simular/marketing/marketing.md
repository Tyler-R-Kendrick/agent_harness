# Simular Marketing

## Audience

- Developers and automation teams that need computer-use agents, not just browser automation.
- Research-aware buyers who compare benchmark performance across WebVoyager, OSWorld, and AndroidWorld-style tasks.
- Teams seeking repeatable long-running workflows with readable execution artifacts.

## Positioning

- Core claim: autonomous computer agents built in the wild, not only in closed labs.
- Category frame: frontier computer-use agent company spanning browsers, desktops, and smartphones.
- Differentiator frame: benchmark leadership plus transparent, modifiable execution through Simulang.

## Customer Model

- Public macOS download, Windows waitlist, and a newly launched web app.
- Developer platform language points to production pipeline and enterprise usage.
- Webhooks and DSL workflows suggest monetization through developer/enterprise automation rather than only consumer subscriptions.

## Who They Capture

- Buyers who think browser-only agents are too narrow.
- Developers who want repeatability and inspectable execution rather than opaque autonomous clicking.
- Teams where app, file, and OS workflows matter as much as web pages.

## Who They Miss

- Users who only need a simple Chrome sidebar or MCP browser bridge.
- Enterprises unwilling to grant broad desktop control without mature policy, audit, and admin controls.
- Buyers skeptical of benchmark-led claims unless they see workflow-specific reliability.
