# OpenClaw Marketing

## Audience

- Self-hosters who want a personal assistant under their control.
- Power users who prefer messaging channels over a dedicated browser or app.
- Developers who want an extensible agent with skills, plugins, Docker, Nix, and local devices.
- Users who want always-on automation for messages, calendars, browsing, scripts, and workflows.

## Positioning

OpenClaw positions itself as the personal AI assistant users run on their own devices. The pitch is not "another chatbot"; it is a local assistant that answers where the user already communicates and can actually do work through skills and device access.

## Customer Model

- Open-source core with community adoption, skill ecosystem growth, and self-hosted deployment.
- Distribution through GitHub, docs, Discord, channel integrations, and third-party guides.
- The customer relationship is closer to developer infrastructure and hobbyist self-hosting than enterprise SaaS.

## Who It Captures

- Users who want a single agent across messaging channels.
- Privacy-minded technical users willing to operate a local Gateway.
- Automation enthusiasts who value extensibility over a polished managed product.
- People who need assistant access from phone and chat surfaces more than from a browser chrome.

## Who It Does Not Capture

- Security-conscious enterprises that need vetted extensions, policy controls, and managed identity.
- Casual users who cannot safely evaluate third-party executable skills.
- Browser-first workflows that require detailed screenshots, DOM state, video, and replayable evidence after each action.
- Teams that need support contracts and deterministic compliance logs.

## Competitive Takeaway

OpenClaw shows that browser and desktop agents are becoming ambient personal infrastructure. `agent-browser` should borrow the always-available feel while avoiding opaque local code execution and preserving page-level evidence.
