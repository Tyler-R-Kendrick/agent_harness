# Skyvern Marketing

## Audience

- Developers and operations teams automating manual browser workflows.
- Teams replacing brittle XPath/CSS-selector scripts with adaptive LLM and vision-based runs.
- Companies that need browser-based workflows for forms, extraction, logged-in apps, and multi-step back-office processes.

## Positioning

- Core claim: automate browser-based workflows with LLMs and computer vision.
- Market frame: traditional browser automation breaks when layouts change; Skyvern can inspect the screen and DOM like a human.
- Product promise: describe the job in natural language, run it through a real Chromium browser, and review artifacts when it completes.

## Customer Model

- Open-source funnel through the AGPL Skyvern repository.
- Managed cloud monetization for teams that need hosted browser sessions, residential proxies, artifacts, and lower operational burden.
- Enterprise opportunity around workflow reliability, credential handling, support, and compliance-sensitive automation.

## Who They Capture

- Engineering teams that want an open-source browser-agent system rather than a closed SaaS-only API.
- Operations-heavy companies with repetitive web tasks and no complete upstream API coverage.
- Developers who need recordings and failure reasons to debug production runs.

## Who They Miss

- End users looking for a polished day-to-day AI browser workspace.
- Teams unwilling to operate or trust agentic browser execution against sensitive logged-in apps.
- Users who want deterministic scripts first and AI only as a narrow fallback.

## Competitive Read

Skyvern is closest to `agent-browser` where both value inspectability and artifact-backed runs. The competitive pressure is highest around workflow/run primitives, but `agent-browser` can differentiate with local-first UX, explicit process traces, and tighter integration into developer workspaces.
