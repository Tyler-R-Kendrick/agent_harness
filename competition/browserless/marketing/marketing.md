# Browserless Marketing

## Audience

- Developers and data teams running scraping, browser automation, screenshots, PDFs, and testing at scale.
- AI-agent builders that need managed browsers, anti-bot handling, CAPTCHA handling, proxies, and browser-session persistence.
- Enterprises that want private deployments or licensed self-hosting rather than a purely public SaaS browser fleet.

## Positioning

- Core claim: scrape and automate any site while getting past CAPTCHAs and bot detectors.
- Category frame: browsers are expensive infrastructure; Browserless removes browser host operations and adds stealth/debugging primitives.
- AI-agent angle: Browserless presents MCP, Browser Use, no-code, and computer-use integrations as ways to expose managed browser capability to agents.

## Customer Model

- Free plan for trial and low-volume experiments.
- Paid plans scale by monthly units, concurrency, max session time, retention, support, and overage rates.
- Enterprise captures private deployment, self-hosting license, custom browser machines, SSO, GPU infrastructure, custom regions, and dedicated support.

## Who They Capture

- Teams with scraping and automation workloads that already know Puppeteer or Playwright.
- AI-agent developers who need browser infrastructure before they need a polished end-user browser UI.
- Compliance-sensitive buyers that still need private hosting or commercial self-hosting.

## Who They Miss

- Users who want local-first interactive browsing with transparent agent traces.
- Teams that do not want to learn BrowserQL or reason about browser-time units.
- Product teams that need a full workspace, chat-agent routing, and human approval UX instead of browser fleet primitives.
