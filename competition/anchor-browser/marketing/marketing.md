# Anchor Browser Marketing

## Audience

- Enterprises and startups building computer-use agents that need to operate logged-in web apps.
- Teams automating processes across disconnected applications that lack complete APIs.
- AI-agent companies that need authentication, browser fleets, CAPTCHA handling, geolocation, and secure account connection.

## Positioning

- Core claim: secure infrastructure for computer-use agents.
- Market frame: browser automations are brittle, costly, blocked, and fragile, especially when authentication is involved.
- Product promise: a managed fleet of humanized Chromium instances that can assume identities, access websites, and run deterministic browser tasks at scale.

## Customer Model

- Free and starter plans for initial adoption, then larger team/growth and enterprise tiers.
- Pricing combines credits per completed task with infrastructure-style browser creation, browser-hour, proxy, and AI-step charges.
- Enterprise expansion centers on compliance, data retention, SSO/RBAC, regions, custom proxies, full stealth, implementation support, and bring-your-own-cloud.

## Who They Capture

- Buyers whose real blocker is authenticated access, not just browser control.
- Companies that need end-user account connection through an embeddable auth flow.
- Teams that distrust pure runtime agents and want deterministic tasks with AI fallback.

## Who They Miss

- Developers who only need local browser automation or open-source primitives.
- End users who want to browse and supervise a personal AI assistant directly.
- Teams unwilling to centralize third-party credentials or browser identities with an infrastructure vendor.

## Competitive Read

Anchor is a strong competitor for `agent-browser` if the target use case is enterprise authenticated automation. `agent-browser` should defend with explicit user approval surfaces, local credential boundaries, auditable process logs, and a clean path from exploratory browser work to checked-in deterministic scripts.
