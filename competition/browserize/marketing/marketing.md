# Browserize Marketing

## Audience

- AI engineers and agent builders who already know Playwright MCP, CDP, and remote browser debugging.
- Small teams that want cheap hosted browser capacity without buying a broader automation suite.
- Developers comparing raw browser infrastructure against Browserbase, Browserless, BrowserCat, Kernel, Steel, and self-hosted Playwright.

## Positioning

- "The AI-First Web Browser" and a "complete browser automation stack" are the core claims.
- The product sells readiness: preconfigured MCP server, CDP server, and NoVNC visual access.
- The price anchor is intentionally simple: free development time, then pay-as-you-go browser hours at $0.10/hour.

## Customer Model

- Product-led developer signup with low entry cost.
- Usage-based infrastructure billing rather than per-seat collaboration pricing.
- Enterprise motion is implied by scale/custom configuration claims, but the public page mainly speaks to builders and teams.

## Who They Capture

- Builders who treat the browser as disposable compute and want a protocol endpoint quickly.
- Teams that do not need a packaged end-user browser UX or no-code workflow builder.
- MCP-native developers who want remote browser sessions that can be watched through NoVNC.

## Who They Miss

- Users who need local authenticated browser state, approval controls, or personal browsing continuity.
- Enterprises that need policy dashboards, audit retention, role controls, and compliance language before sending agents to the web.
- Non-technical operators who want a visual automation builder rather than MCP/CDP endpoints.

## Competitive Notes For agent-browser

- Browserize validates that MCP-ready browser capacity is becoming commoditized.
- The counter-position is evidence and authority, not raw browser hosting: local state boundaries, run history, screenshots/video, approvals, and recovery controls.
