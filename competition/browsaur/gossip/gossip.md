# Browsaur Gossip

## Positive Signals

- The public site provides unusually specific claims: 31/31 bot-detection tests, 50M+ pages scraped, 3,000+ developers, and concrete entry pricing.
- It has an explicit self-hosting story for teams that want to avoid usage lock-in.
- Testimonials emphasize migration from Steel, Bright Data, BrowserStack, Browserbase, and proxy-maintenance work.

## Negative Signals

- Many proof points are vendor-presented. Treat them as claims until independently reproduced.
- A one-person-operation FAQ answer is candid, but it creates continuity and support risk for serious production users.
- The aggressive comparison copy could age poorly if competitors improve stealth or pricing.

## Category Chatter

- Browser automation communities repeatedly complain that headless and screenshot-driven approaches are brittle against modern sites.
- Cost chatter around browser agents highlights that cheap models and cheap browser hours can become expensive when agents loop on selectors or retries.
- Scraping communities increasingly want OAuth, MCP, or remote control surfaces that let web Claude, ChatGPT, Cursor, or phone-based agents reach the browser on a desk.

## Bug And UX Risks To Watch

- Real Chrome does not remove the need for workflow-level retries, consent, and stop conditions.
- Persistent profiles can preserve useful auth but also preserve sensitive state.
- Residential routing claims may invite compliance review and acceptable-use friction.
- Self-hosting on Mac hardware is attractive but can become an operations burden for teams without device management.
